# Bellwether Reef

## Description

Bellwether Reef lies half a kilometer south of Port Kestrel, where coral and kelp have grown through the roofs of the drowned town's municipal buildings. The reef takes its name from the old bell tower wedged below the surface. In calm weather, divers can hear the bell knock faintly against its cracked frame as the current shifts.

Visibility is poor after storms. Silt rises in brown sheets, loose rebar hides under weed, and the current pulls hard toward the intake channels. Salvage crews avoid the eastern shelf unless the tide is slack and the weather has been kind for two days.

## History

Before the flood years, Bellwether was the civic center of the old town. Its basement records room was built deep and dry. After the sea took it, the Harbor Authority sealed several chambers and marked the site unstable. Theo later discovered that one sealed chamber had power, cooling lines, and data traffic routed through abandoned storm-grid conduits.

## Culture & Customs

Divers treat the reef with professional superstition. They knock twice on the bell frame before entering the municipal ruins and leave a strip of colored tape on the guide line for anyone who died underwater. Mara calls this nonsense and still ties the tape.

## Notable Features

- **The Bell Tower** - a cracked navigation hazard used as a dive marker
- **Records Basement** - sealed pre-flood municipal archive, now repurposed
- **Intake Channel** - dangerous current path feeding Port Kestrel's storm-grid turbines
- **Eastern Shelf** - storm-damaged reef face where the archive capsule is exposed

## Current State

A recent storm has torn open part of the eastern shelf. What looked like reef stone from above is now clearly manufactured: a pressure-sealed chamber, Authority-grade bolts, and a data spine still blinking after three years underwater.
