# Chapter 1: The Bell Under the Reef

## Outline

1. **Opening** - Mara dives Bellwether Reef after a storm, checking damage near the old bell tower [Location: bellwether-reef] [Arc beat: storm exposes sealed chamber]
2. **The wrong sound** - She hears the bell strike underwater and finds manufactured plating under fresh reef breakage [Foreshadowing: bell failsafe]
3. **The lantern** - Mara cuts open the chamber and recovers a pressure-sealed memory capsule marked with Theo's shorthand [System: afterimage-archive]
4. **Playback** - Back on Dock Six, Mara triggers the lantern and hears Theo's hidden recording [Character: theo-quill] [Arc beat: Theo becomes a witness]
5. **Decision** - Theo warns that the archive was lit during the Blackout Night. Mara chooses to keep the capsule from the Harbor Authority [Theme: truth-after-loss]

The bell was ringing under the reef.

Mara heard it through thirty feet of water, not as sound exactly, but as a dull knock that entered through her ribs and settled behind her teeth. She stopped moving. Silt curled around her mask. Above her, the morning light broke into green strips on the surface, then vanished as a cloud crossed the sun.

The bell knocked again.

It had no business moving. The old tower had been wedged sideways in the municipal roof since before Mara was born, its bronze mouth packed with coral and weed. Divers used it as a marker. They tapped it with a knife when visibility went bad. It did not ring by itself.

"I heard it," Mara said.

Her helmet radio hissed. Dock Six answered in Juno's bored voice. "You heard reef noise."

"Reef noise does not keep time."

"Nothing keeps time down there. That is why I am topside and pretty."

Mara let the line go quiet. Juno talked when she was worried, and the dive had given her reasons. Last night's storm had torn two algae rafts loose, snapped a turbine brace, and pushed half the harbor's trash south into Bellwether Reef. Mara was supposed to check the intake channel, mark loose metal, and come up before the tide turned ugly.

She should have been at the channel already.

The bell knocked a third time.

Mara angled her lamp toward the tower. Brown silt moved in long curtains. Kelp dragged at the old stonework. She followed the sound past a window frame furred white with tube worms and down along the eastern shelf, where the storm had peeled away a slab of reef.

Under the break, something blinked.

Not fish shine. Not glass. A small blue status light pulsed behind a film of salt and algae, regular as a machine that still expected someone to answer it.

Mara hooked one glove into a crack and pulled herself closer. Her breath sounded too loud in the helmet. The exposed surface was not stone. It was ceramic plating, Authority gray under the growth, sealed with bolts stamped too cleanly to be pre-flood. Someone had built a door into the drowned records basement and let coral dress it up as geology.

"Juno," she said, "log a structure on the east shelf."

"Define structure."

"Authority plating. Live power."

The radio went quiet for half a second. "Come up."

"Send me the bolt map for grid housings."

"Mara."

"The tide gives me nine minutes."

"Seven."

"Then send fast."

The file hit her wrist display: a jittering schematic, half wrong for the angle, good enough for the tool choice. Mara clipped the cutter from her belt. It was meant for storm mesh, not whatever this was, but the first bolt surrendered after three seconds of blue-white heat. The second fought her. The third spun loose by hand, which was worse.

Somebody had opened this door before.

The bell knocked again, closer now, though the tower was behind her.

Mara froze. Her lamp flickered once. In that brief dark, the blue status light doubled against the inside of her mask. She saw two points where there should have been one.

"Current is shifting," Juno said. "You have four minutes."

Mara slid her pry bar into the seam and leaned her weight onto it. The plate gave with a soft cough of trapped air. Black water breathed out from the chamber beyond.

Inside waited a rack of pressure capsules, each about the length of her forearm. Most had gone dark. One still glowed, its casing scratched with a mark she knew so well that her hand slipped on the pry bar.

TQ.

Theo had carved those letters into tools, battery shells, wet concrete, the underside of their mother's kitchen table. Always too small, always where someone would find them later and swear at him.

Mara stared at the capsule until Juno's voice cut hard through the hiss.

"Mara. Up. Now."

She took the glowing capsule, clipped it inside her salvage bag, and kicked away from the chamber. The reef pulled at her fins. The guide line trembled. Behind her, from somewhere in the drowned building, the bell struck once more.

This time it sounded like a warning.

---

Dock Six smelled of wet rope, frying onions, and hot plastic. Mara came up shivering despite the thermal suit. Juno hauled her onto the platform by the back of her harness and swore at her with real effort, which meant she had been afraid.

"Seven minutes means seven," Juno said.

"I was back in six and forty."

"I am going to write that on your marker."

Mara unclipped the salvage bag instead of answering. Water streamed through the mesh. The capsule lay inside like a caught fish, dark now except for the faint blue seam along one side.

Juno stopped swearing.

"That Authority?"

"No registry stamp."

"That is not what I asked."

Mara wiped algae from the casing with her thumb. TQ remained, cut deep enough to survive three years underwater.

Juno saw it. Her mouth changed. "Mara."

"Get me a reader."

"If that is Council hardware, we call a witness."

"You are a witness."

"I mean someone with a badge."

Mara looked up at the Council Spire across the harbor. It rose white and dry above the central pier, all clean windows and storm flags. Three years ago, a man in that tower had read Theo's name into the public band and made him into the reason twelve people did not come home.

"No badges," Mara said.

Juno held her stare, then spat over the side. "My cousin has an old therapy visor."

"Working?"

"Illegal."

"That was not what I asked."

It took twenty minutes to fetch the visor, five to splice its rotten strap with cable ties, and another ten for Juno to stop listing the ways this could fry Mara's brain. The capsule accepted the connection before Mara finished seating the plug. Its seam brightened. The visor display filled with static, then a smell hit her that had no place on the dock: ozone, engine oil, Theo's cheap mint gum.

Mara gripped the workbench.

The static thinned.

Theo's voice came through, close and breathless. "Mar, if you found this, first, I am sorry. Second, do not plug it into a Council reader. Third, I know you already ignored both of those instructions in spirit."

Juno made a small sound.

Mara could not move.

There was no image at first. Only fragments: a red warning light, rain hammering sheet metal, Theo breathing hard enough to clip the recorder. Then his face appeared at the edge of the frame, too close, younger than she remembered and exactly as tired.

"The archive is real," he said. "Bellwether basement, eastern shelf. Venn's people have been routing power through the storm grid. Therapy hardware, but wrong. It is not treatment anymore."

Mara's vision pinched at the edges. She forced herself to breathe through her nose. In for four. Hold. Out.

Theo looked away from the recorder. Somewhere behind him, a siren rose and fell.

"They are storing people, Mar. Not files. People. Pieces of them, anyway. I have names, but if I put the index here and this gets opened by the wrong reader, Venn buries the whole reef. So you get the key, not the door."

"Theo," Mara said, uselessly.

His recorded face turned back as if he had heard her.

"The Blackout Night was not me. I need you to know that before anything else. The archive was lit during the blackout. Lit. While Dock Three was drowning and the backup grid was starving, that basement had power."

Juno whispered a curse.

The visor warmed against Mara's face. For a second she was not on Dock Six. She was in Theo's body, tasting blood where he had bitten his tongue, feeling cold water climb past his ankles. Panic struck so sharply that her knees bent.

Juno grabbed her shoulder. "Take it off."

Mara tore the visor free. The dock snapped back around her: gulls, rope, Juno's hand, the gray noon harbor shifting under them. Across the water, the Council Spire flashed its storm-clean windows at the sun.

Juno stared at Mara's face. "Your eyes."

"What?"

"There was..." Juno swallowed. "For a second, there were two lights in them."

Mara looked down at the capsule. The blue seam had gone dark, but Theo's mark remained under her thumb.

Three years of silence sat between them. Three years of lowered voices when Mara entered a room. Three years of people touching her sleeve as if pity gave them the right.

She closed her hand around the capsule.

"Log the dive as intake debris," she said.

Juno shook her head. "Venn will ask."

"Let him."

"Mara."

She put the dead visor back on the bench and looked once more toward the white tower.

"He left me the key," she said. "I am going to find the door."
